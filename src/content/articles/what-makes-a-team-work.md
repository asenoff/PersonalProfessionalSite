I have always said that I work with people, rather than with technology.

Over the years, I have been part of six different teams: two highly performant, two average, and two dysfunctional. That experience has made me question what actually differentiates a close-knit, effective team from a chaotic, underperforming one.

Here is my honest answer.

## Process Helps, but People Decide

I used to be, and largely still am, a firm believer in Agile philosophy. Its structure and result-oriented nature match my inner drive. But I have seen it both succeed with minimal rituals and fail despite serious implementation effort in large environments.

The difference was not the process.

Agile worked when people were genuinely engaged in their work, when they were proactive, responsible, and involved beyond the mechanics of the process. When that was missing, Agile became just a structure with shallow foundations.

> People matter more than process. A weak team can hollow out a good process, while a strong team can perform even in a basic setup.

## What I Have Seen in Real Teams

In one of the best teams I have worked with, there was a respectful, helpful, and growth-oriented atmosphere. People took initiative, supported each other, and moved the work forward without needing constant coordination.

In contrast, I have also been part of a large team where the environment promoted passiveness. People avoided responsibility, tried to stay invisible, and rarely took ownership. The result was friction, delays, and wasted energy.

> A good team creates synergy. A bad one creates friction.

## Freedom Within Boundaries

Over time, I have found that the most effective environments balance freedom and boundaries.

- **The boundaries should be strategic.** Clear goals, shared values, and direction.
- **The freedom should be operational.** How people approach their work, solve problems, and contribute.

Without boundaries, there is chaos.

Without freedom, there is no motivation, and the cost of micromanagement becomes enormous.

Interestingly, this is something I have also learned outside of work. My four-year-old daughter has been a surprisingly good teacher in this regard. Giving freedom within clearly defined boundaries, with respect and acceptance, leads to growth and initiative. The same pattern holds in teams.

## Ownership and Collaboration

In my role, I have mostly been a contributor, often with ownership on the technical side. From that position, I try to be proactive and constructive without imposing decisions.

When I step into other domains, I usually do it by asking questions and sharing ideas. Often, I discover there is more context than I initially understood, and I adjust my perspective. Respecting the boundaries of other people's expertise matters.

Clear ownership matters because it reduces confusion and helps work move forward. Overstepping can create tension, but lack of ownership creates delay and ambiguity. A working balance is essential.

## When Things Break Down

Even with the best intentions, collaboration can fail.

In one case, repeated delays and unkept commitments from a colleague started affecting my work. I tried to push things forward, but frustration built up over time. Eventually, it affected communication and led to conflict.

That was a useful lesson. Even when behavior appears controlled, the underlying disposition, whether frustration, anger, or fear, eventually shows through. It cannot be hidden for long.

What works better is:

- addressing issues early
- speaking clearly
- staying respectful while still being direct

## Feedback and Growth

Another important aspect of teamwork is handling criticism.

For a long time, feedback felt personal. Over time, I learned to separate my identity from the work I produce.

> I am not my code.

That shift changes everything. Code reviews become constructive conversations. Negative feedback becomes a source of improvement rather than resistance. It leads to better code and better collaboration.

## Communication and Trust

My training in nonviolent communication has helped me understand both myself and others better.

It improved how I express concerns, how I listen, and how I approach conflict. More importantly, it made me more transparent in my communication. That transparency builds trust.

And trust is what allows teams to function well under pressure.

## The Impact on Engineering Work

This is not just about people. It directly affects the software.

**Good teams:**

- reduce bug rates
- improve communication and clarity
- handle absence or pressure without collapsing
- produce more maintainable systems

**Bad teams:**

- create hidden assumptions and miscommunication
- increase technical debt
- waste time and energy in coordination
- make systems harder to maintain over time

## What a Team Really Is

At a minimum, a team requires:

- a common goal
- shared values

Without those, it is just an aggregation of people.

With them, it becomes something more.

## Closing Thought

What lasts in a work setting is not only the software or the financial outcome, but the quality of the collaboration behind it.

In my experience, good collaboration is at least as important to the success of a software project as a sound technical stack or architecture.

It cannot be forced or engineered directly. But it can grow through respect, clarity, responsibility, and a shared willingness to contribute.

And that is what ultimately makes a team.
